Step 2: System Heuristics Critique

  • Visibility of System Status: The loading indicator on page after selecting link lets users know that the website is processing their query
  • Match between System and Real World: Menu options discoverable immediately at the top of the page, though editorials are obscured by vague “Quill” tab
  • User Control and Freedom: User can easily come back to homepage by clicking hyperlink title page
  • Consistency and Standards: Pages are clearly labeled with the pathway at the top left, so it is easy for users to know where they are and how to navigate away from the page
  • Error Prevention: When no results appear, user is notified and asked to refine search; no correction suggestions though
  • Recognition rather than Recall: The website's tree and root structure is clear; axes of time and topic are structured in a familiar way, and this structure invites readers to consider the history of the website's publications.
  • Flexibility and Efficiency of Use: Its logical system structure allows ease of use. There is a shortcut to return to top of page at the bottom of page, as well as shortcuts to topical and popular articles.
  • Aesthetic and Minimalist Design: The webpage is simple, not overwhelming with information, with only ten articles per page
  • Help Users Recognize, Diagnose, and Recover from Errors: Notification and reproduction of failed searches can alert users that they may need to reword their query, but the search engine itself does not provide potential alternative spellings or suggest alternative searches.
  • Help and Documentation: There is no help page or FAQs, though there are links to contact members of The Tower's publication.

Step 3: Summary

Strengths: Recognition rather than Recall & Consistency and Standards

Overall, the system’s greatest strengths are its structure and its clear labelings for articles. The website has a logical tree-like structure which can be accessed along axes of time and topic. These axes are arranged very much like a classic grid design, making navigation of the website very intuitive. Each page is labeled with the pathway to the current page, so it is easy to know where you are on any given page and easy to navigate back to where you had been before. Each individual article is also tagged with the topic it would fall under (i.e. “Feature,” “News,” “Politics,” etc.), so it is easy to understand at a glance what sort of information the item will contain.

Weaknesses: Match Between System and Real World & Error Prevention

Overall, the system’s greatest weaknesses are its lack of search suggestions and some of its menu organization. There is a tab for an item called “Quill,” and it is not clear to the user immediately that this might be an editorial section. The editorial section is listed beneath Quill, and it is not immediately clear how these items are different or why they would need to be separated. When using the search engine, misspellings are not supported with alternative suggestions.

Step 4: Survey and Observation Documents Submitted Separately

Step 5: Compare Results of Part 1 and Part 2

The Tower website is a useful resource for users interested in learning more about campus attitudes and relevant news for Catholic University students. Since its creation in 2015, it has maintained an easily navigable archive of electronic articles. From news to op-eds, The Tower website gives readers a nuanced look at the Catholic University campus community over the past ten years. Its organization, which emphasizes the website's digital archive, emphasizes its invitation to readers to consider the history of the publication, and it makes the navigation of its archives simple and straight-forward. Its readily available metadata on each article, inscribing relevant generic information for these articles, allows the reader to quickly find the type of article to satisfy their query.

However, The Tower website does not convey the rich history of the publication, which was established in 1922. In order to view articles from before 2015, users need to have access to JSTOR, and there is not a conspicuous link to this specific archive on The Tower website. When asked to search for topics related to World War 2 and the Vietnam War, the user is not guided to The Tower's rich archive, but is limited to more recent articles that mention World War 2 or the recent passing of Vietnam veteran, John McCain. The website fails as a tool for excavating opinions and perspectives on historical issues, though its landmark transition to a completely digitized archive may serve future readers interested in campus attitudes toward the Covid-19 pandemic, for instance.

I would recommend that the website, which touts its status as a 100 year old publication, at least offer readers an easy way to connect to its rich archive. Ideally, the writers for the website could take up the work of transcribing the documents in The Tower's archive to provide more readable historical texts for its users. Much like the work of Project Gutenberg, the publishers of The Tower could enhance access to the publication's history by rendering that history into the same very readable format of the current website. This work would also make the publication more accessible to users with reading difficulties, as the PDFs of microfilm articles from the historic Tower publication can be challenging to read, and there is no way for a user's machine to dictate the content through an audiofile.
